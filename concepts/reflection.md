# Reflection

## Definition

Reflection is the capacity to review and reason about one's own prior outputs, reasoning processes, or knowledge state. In cognitive science, metacognition — thinking about thinking — is the broader framework within which reflection sits. In BDM, reflection is defined more narrowly as a computational process: given a new output candidate, compare it against prior outputs and flag or correct inconsistencies.

---

## Why It Matters

Systems that generate outputs without reviewing them for consistency can drift over time. A single session with a well-calibrated system may appear coherent. But across many sessions and many topics, without any mechanism for consistency checking, contradictions accumulate. The system may assert X in one context and not-X in another, with no mechanism to detect or resolve the conflict.

Reflection is the mechanism that addresses this. It is not about achieving philosophical self-awareness — it is about implementing a structured review pass that catches detectable inconsistencies before they are surfaced to users.

---

## Possible Software Representation

A reflection module in BDM would be implemented as a post-processing step in the output pipeline:

1. A candidate output is generated by the LLM
2. The reflection module retrieves prior outputs on the same or related topics from the memory store
3. The reflection module compares the candidate output against prior outputs
4. If a potential inconsistency is detected, the module produces a reflection report:
   - `consistent` — no contradictions detected, output approved
   - `inconsistent` — contradiction detected, flag for revision
   - `uncertain` — comparison inconclusive, flag for review
5. If inconsistent, the module optionally generates a revised output or passes the flag upstream

The comparison step could be:
- **Rule-based:** explicit contradiction detection (e.g., "A" vs. "not A" on the same fact)
- **LLM-assisted:** a secondary prompt asks the LLM to compare the candidate against prior outputs and assess consistency
- **Hybrid:** rule-based detection for flagrant contradictions, LLM-assisted for subtle ones

---

## Open Questions

- Should reflection be blocking (holds output until review completes) or asynchronous (flags inconsistencies after the fact)?
- How do we distinguish a legitimate update to a prior position from an inconsistency?
- What is the computational cost of reflection, and at what scale does it become impractical?
- Can reflection be evaluated reliably? What metrics can distinguish good reflection from bad?
- Is there a risk of reflection causing output to become overly conservative — consistently hedging rather than committing to positions?

---

## Relation to BDM

Reflection is the third layer in BDM's architecture. It depends on the memory layer (to retrieve prior outputs for comparison) and the attention layer (to surface relevant prior outputs efficiently). It feeds into the learning loop: when reflection detects an inconsistency and a revision is made, that revision may trigger an update to the memory store.

Reflection is also closely related to the self-model layer: the self-model may represent the system's known consistency record, and reflection events may update it.
